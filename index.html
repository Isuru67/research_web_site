About the Project

Empowering paralyzed individuals with intelligent robotics.

Paralysis affects millions worldwide, stripping away independence for even the simplest daily tasks. Existing assistive devices are often expensive, fragile, or lack intelligence. We built something different.

The Problem

Independence lost to paralysis.

Individuals with paralysis or severe motor impairment face immense challenges in performing basic daily activities such as eating, drinking, or communicating needs to caregivers. Current assistive devices are prohibitively expensive, lack real-time intelligence, and fail to adapt to patient behavior over time.

Emergency situations go undetected, caregiver response is slow, and patients' mental and physical health deteriorates without continuous monitoring. The gap between need and affordable, intelligent solution is vast.

Our Solution

An affordable, AI-powered assistive arm.

Our system integrates four intelligent sub-components into a unified assistive robotic platform. A Raspberry Pi 5-powered arm uses YOLOv8 to detect and deliver snacks autonomously. A low-cost smart glove translates hand gestures into precise arm commands.

A camera-based module detects emotions, objects, and dangerous body movements, sending real-time emergency alerts to caregivers. A behavioral analytics engine predicts patient health trends — enabling proactive, compassionate care at a fraction of current costs.

Literature Review

The research foundation.

A rigorous survey of assistive robotics, gesture recognition, emotion detection, and behavioral health monitoring systems that shaped our approach.

01

Assistive Robotic Arms for Motor-Impaired Individuals

Commercial assistive arms such as JACO [1] and Kinova Gen3 [2] demonstrate the viability of robotic feeding and manipulation for paralyzed users but remain prohibitively expensive (>$30,000). Research on shared-control robotics [3] shows promise in partially automating arm movements guided by user intent, yet relies on EEG or eye-tracking interfaces unavailable in low-resource settings. Studies on low-cost arms using servo motors and microcontrollers [4] achieve functional reach-and-grasp but lack AI-driven object detection and adaptive delivery. A YOLOv8-based pick-and-place study [5] demonstrated 91% object detection accuracy in cluttered environments, directly informing our biscuit-delivery module design.

References

  2. Kinova Inc., "Gen3 Robotic Arm — Technical Specifications," [Online]. Available: https://www.kinovarobotics.com.
  3. A. Herlant, R. Holladay and S. Srinivasa, "Assistive Teleoperation of Robot Arms via Automatic Time-Optimal Mode Switching," ACM/IEEE HRI, 2016, pp. 35–42.
  4. P. Kumar and R. Sharma, "Low-Cost Servo-Based Robotic Arm for Assistive Applications," IEEE TENCON, 2022, pp. 88–93.
  5. L. Zhang, Q. Li and H. Wu, "YOLOv8-Based Object Detection for Robotic Pick-and-Place in Assistive Contexts," Sensors, vol. 23, no. 11, 2023.
02

Gesture Recognition & Wearable Glove Controllers

Flex-sensor gloves [6] and IMU-based gesture systems [7] have been widely explored for robotic control, achieving up to 96% gesture classification accuracy. However, most systems map gestures only to predefined coarse actions and lack auxiliary signal encoding for yes/no responses or emergency states. Soft glove designs [8] prioritize comfort but compromise sensor fidelity in clinical settings. Recent work on low-cost flex sensor arrays [9] combined with ML classifiers achieved real-time gesture inference at <50ms latency on embedded hardware. Our smart glove innovates by incorporating emergency deactivation gestures and yes/no communication alongside standard arm control — a multi-signal approach not found in existing literature.

References
  1. S. Sharma and A. Gupta, "Flex Sensor Based Gesture Recognition Glove for Robotic Control," IEEE ICIEV, 2021, pp. 1–6.
  2. D. Kim, J. Park and Y. Lee, "IMU-Based Real-Time Hand Gesture Classification for HRI," Robotics and Autonomous Systems, vol. 148, 2022.
  3. X. Liu et al., "Soft Wearable Glove for Dexterous Robotic Hand Teleoperation," Advanced Intelligent Systems, vol. 5, no. 3, 2023.
  4. R. Fernando and K. Perera, "Low-Cost Flex Sensor Array for Embedded Gesture Inference," IEEE ICIIS, 2022, pp. 201–206.
03

Emotion Detection & Real-Time Emergency Alerting

Facial emotion recognition using deep CNNs [10] achieves up to 97% accuracy on benchmarks such as FER-2013, but clinical deployment in real-time caregiving contexts is rare. Fall detection systems using pose estimation [11] and accelerometers [12] have been validated in elderly care settings with recall rates above 90%, yet rarely integrate body tremor analysis or multi-signal risk prioritization. Alert routing frameworks [13] for caregiver notification typically rely on simple threshold triggers. No existing system simultaneously detects emotion, object presence, body fall, and tremors while applying risk-level prioritization to trigger graduated caregiver alerts — the core novelty of our detection module.

References
  1. S. Li, W. Deng and J. Du, "Reliable Crowdsourcing and Deep Locality-Preserving Learning for Expression Recognition," CVPR, 2017.
  2. H. Chen, G. Bhanu and S. Bhanu, "Human Fall Detection Using 3D Multi-Stream CNN," IEEE Sensors Journal, vol. 20, no. 7, 2020.
  3. M. Dubowsky et al., "Accelerometer-Based Fall Detection in Elderly Patients: A Systematic Review," Sensors, vol. 22, no. 5, 2022.
  4. J. Park and S. Kim, "Priority-Based Alert Routing for Assisted Living Systems," IEEE IoT Journal, vol. 9, no. 12, 2022.
04

Behavioral Pattern Recognition for Predictive Health

Long-term behavioral monitoring systems [14] using passive sensing have demonstrated the ability to predict cognitive decline and physical deterioration in older adults, with detection sensitivity of 82–89%. Interaction logging in assistive robotics [15] for usability analysis is well-established but rarely linked to health prediction models. Smart home activity recognition [16] using time-series neural networks achieves strong results for routine classification. However, integrating behavioral telemetry from a robotic feeding system specifically — tracking request frequency, hand effort, and consumption patterns — for predictive health modeling in paralysis care represents a novel contribution absent from current literature.

References
  1. R. Rantz et al., "Using Sensor Networks and Machine Learning to Detect and Monitor Decline in Older Adults," Gerontechnology, vol. 14, no. 1, 2015.
  2. L. Mataric, N. Eriksson and J. Feil-Seifer, "Socially Assistive Robotics for Post-Stroke Rehabilitation," Rehabilitation Robotics, 2007, pp. 241–256.
  3. Y. Chen, K. Zheng and X. Liu, "LSTM-Based Activity Recognition from Wearable Sensors for Health Monitoring," IEEE JBHI, vol. 27, no. 4, 2023.
Research Gaps

Four gaps, one integrated answer.

No Affordable Intelligent Assistive Arm

Existing assistive robotic arms are high-cost commercial products without integrated AI object detection, limiting accessibility for most patients and care facilities.

Gesture Gloves Lack Multi-Signal Encoding

Current glove controllers map gestures only to arm movements, with no provision for yes/no communication, emergency distress, or safety deactivation signals.

No Unified Emotion + Motion Emergency System

Existing systems detect either emotion or falls independently. No solution combines emotion, body tremor, object detection, and risk-prioritized caregiver alerting in one pipeline.

Behavioral Data Not Used for Health Prediction

Robotic system interaction logs are never used to infer patient health trends. Predictive health monitoring integrated into a feeding robot is entirely novel in the literature.

Our Objectives

Four pillars of assistive intelligence.

Each sub-component of our system addresses a specific, critical need for paralysis care — from physical assistance to predictive health intelligence.

01

Autonomous Biscuit Detection & Delivery via Robotic Arm

Design and implement an affordable Raspberry Pi 5-based robotic arm that uses YOLOv8 to detect biscuit availability and count, then autonomously picks and delivers a biscuit using servo motors. MediaPipe hand tracking and a VL53L0X proximity sensor ensure safe, precise release only when the user's hand is correctly positioned. Voice commands and mobile app integration provide flexible control.

02

Smart Glove for Intuitive Gesture-Based Arm Control

Develop a low-cost wearable glove that converts raw sensor data from flex sensors into meaningful gesture inputs, mapping these gestures to arm actions including movement, picking, and safe deactivation. Extend the glove's communication capability to encode yes/no responses and emergency requests, enabling a multi-purpose human-robot communication interface for paralyzed users.

03

Real-Time Object, Emotion & Body Movement Detection with Emergency Alerts

Build a camera-based detection pipeline that identifies objects (glasses, biscuits), recognizes emotional states (happiness, sadness, distress), and tracks body movements including falls and tremors to assess risk levels. Deliver risk-prioritized real-time alerts to caregivers during emergency events, enabling faster and more targeted care response.

04

Behavioral Pattern Recognition & Predictive Health Monitoring

Monitor and analyze long-term patient interactions with the robotic system — including request frequency, consumption timing, hand effort, and daily routines. Apply behavioral analytics to predict patient health status: reduced activity signals potential decline while increased activity suggests recovery, enabling proactive caregiver intervention and improved patient outcomes.

Assistive Robotic Arm System

Biscuit Detection & Autonomous Delivery

A Raspberry Pi 5-powered robotic arm equipped with a camera and YOLOv8 model to detect biscuit availability and count. Upon detection, servo motors autonomously pick and deliver a biscuit to the user. MediaPipe hand tracking combined with a VL53L0X proximity sensor ensures the biscuit is released only when the user's hand is correctly positioned — preventing drops and ensuring safety.

Voice commands trigger the system, with additional control via a connected mobile device for caregiver oversight.

Novelty First low-cost embedded robotic feeding system combining real-time YOLOv8 object detection with proximity-guided delivery and voice command triggering — making autonomous assistive feeding accessible beyond expensive commercial solutions.

Smart Gesture Control Glove

Wearable Gesture-to-Command Interface

A wearable smart glove that converts raw flex sensor and IMU data into meaningful gesture inputs, mapping each gesture to specific robotic arm actions — moving the arm, picking items, or safely deactivating the system. Gesture classification runs in real-time on embedded hardware with sub-50ms latency, enabling fluid, intuitive control for users with limited motor function.

The glove sends commands wirelessly to the robotic arm and doubles as a communication interface, encoding yes/no responses and emergency distress signals.

Novelty A low-cost glove that goes beyond standard robotic control to encode multi-modal signals — arm commands, yes/no communication, and an emergency safety deactivation gesture — in a single wearable device.

Emotion & Emergency Detection

Vision-Based Detection & Alert System

A camera-based AI pipeline that simultaneously identifies objects (glasses, biscuits), recognizes emotional states (happiness, sadness, distress), and tracks body movements including falls and tremors to compute risk levels. The system applies a risk-prioritization engine that decides alert severity and routes notifications to caregivers in real time.

Multi-modal fusion enables the system to distinguish between routine activity and genuine emergency, reducing false alarms while accelerating true emergency response.

Methodology

How we built RoboArm.

Our methodology adopts an agile, modular development approach where each sub-component is designed, prototyped, and tested independently before system-level integration. Hardware prototyping uses low-cost components (Raspberry Pi 5, servo motors, flex sensors) to ensure affordability and replicability in resource-constrained care settings.

  • 1 Robotic Arm Module — Raspberry Pi 5 runs YOLOv8 inference on a camera feed for biscuit detection. Servo control logic handles pick-and-place, with MediaPipe and VL53L0X providing safe delivery confirmation before releasing.
  • 2 Smart Glove Module — Flex sensors and IMU output raw readings that are preprocessed and classified by an on-device ML model. Classified gestures are mapped to arm commands and additional signals (SOS, yes/no) transmitted wirelessly.
  • 3 Detection & Alert Module — A multi-model vision pipeline performs simultaneous emotion recognition, object detection, fall detection, and tremor analysis. Risk scores are computed and alert notifications are dispatched via mobile app or SMS to caregivers in real time.
  • 4 Behavioral Analytics Module — Interaction events are logged to a time-series database. Consumption patterns, request frequencies, and activity levels are analyzed using sequence models to generate health-trend predictions and caregiver dashboards.
